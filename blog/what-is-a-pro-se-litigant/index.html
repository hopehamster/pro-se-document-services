What Is a Pro Se Litigant? Your Right to Represent Yourself in Nevada Courts

January 27, 2026 · 5 min read

Millions of Americans navigate civil court every year without hiring an attorney. They draft their own complaints, file their own motions, and appear in court on their own behalf. If you are considering doing the same — or you have already started — understanding what pro se means, what courts expect from you, and where professional document preparation fits into the picture is the essential starting point.

Defining Pro Se

The Latin phrase pro se simply means "for oneself." When you represent yourself in a legal proceeding without retaining a licensed attorney, you are proceeding pro se. You may also see the term pro per, short for propria persona — both terms are used interchangeably in Nevada courts and mean the same thing.

Pro se representation is a legally recognized right in civil proceedings. At the federal level, 28 U.S.C. § 1654 explicitly states that parties may plead and conduct their own cases personally. Nevada's state courts similarly allow and routinely process pro se filings across all civil divisions — Clark County District Court, Justice Court, and the U.S. District Court of Nevada all handle a significant volume of self-represented litigants each year.

Who Files Pro Se — and Why

The number of pro se litigants in Nevada courts is substantial. In many civil case categories — landlord-tenant disputes, debt collection defense, small claims matters, probate proceedings, and civil rights filings — a significant portion of cases involve at least one self-represented party. People choose to represent themselves for a variety of legitimate reasons:

  • Attorney fees are prohibitive. Full civil litigation representation in Nevada can run $250 to $500 per hour, with retainers starting in the thousands before a single document is filed. For many disputes, those fees exceed the amount at stake.
  • The matter is procedural, not strategic. Many civil cases turn entirely on whether the right documents were filed correctly, on time, and in the right court. The legal "strategy" is straightforward — what's complex is the paperwork.
  • The amount in dispute doesn't justify the cost of counsel. A $12,000 contract dispute handled at $350/hour can cost more in legal fees than the judgment itself.
  • The person is well-informed and prefers direct control. Clients who understand their own facts often decide they are capable of presenting those facts — they simply need professionally prepared documents to do it effectively.

What Courts Expect from Pro Se Litigants

Here is the reality that every self-represented litigant needs to understand clearly: Nevada courts extend some patience to pro se parties in how they read documents, but they do not waive procedural rules on your behalf.

The U.S. Supreme Court's ruling in Haines v. Kerner established that courts should read pro se filings liberally and hold them to a less stringent standard than documents prepared by attorneys. That is genuine and meaningful. What it does not mean is that your filing can be sloppy, late, or procedurally noncompliant and still move forward.

Your complaint must still state a valid cause of action. Your motion must still comply with local formatting rules. Your response to a motion must still be filed within the deadline. A complaint filed in the wrong court, a motion missing required notice language, or a response submitted a single day late can be fatal to your case regardless of the merits of your underlying claims.

The Role of Document Preparation Services

This is precisely the gap that professional document preparation services exist to fill. A Registered Document Preparer under Nevada's NRS Chapter 240A is not an attorney — they cannot give you legal advice, tell you what claims to pursue, or appear in court on your behalf. What they can do is ensure that the documents you have decided to file are prepared to professional court standards: correctly formatted, properly captioned, procedurally compliant with local rules, and filing-ready.

The difference between a rejected filing and an accepted one is often entirely a matter of precision in drafting. Margins, caption format, certification language, service documentation, exhibit formatting — these are technical requirements that have nothing to do with the legal strength of your position and everything to do with whether your case moves forward at all.

When you work with a professional document preparer with genuine "Big Law" paralegal experience, you are not getting a typist. You are getting a specialist who knows exactly what a Nevada district court clerk — or a federal court filing system — requires before it will accept a document into the record.

The Bottom Line

Pro se litigation is a legitimate, often practical choice — especially for procedural matters, probate proceedings, civil complaints with clear facts, and federal filings where the paperwork complexity outweighs the legal complexity. The key to succeeding as a pro se litigant is treating document quality as non-negotiable.

You have the right to represent yourself. Make sure your documents represent you well.
