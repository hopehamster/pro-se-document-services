What Are Letters Testamentary and Why Do You Need Them?

March 19, 2026 · 6 min read

If you have been named executor in a will or appointed administrator of an estate in Nevada, you will quickly discover that one document unlocks everything else: Letters Testamentary (or, in cases without a will, Letters of Administration). Without this court-issued authorization, you cannot effectively access, manage, or transfer estate assets — no matter how clearly the will names you or how certain your family is about who should handle the estate. Understanding what Letters Testamentary are, how to obtain them, and how to use them is essential knowledge for any estate administrator in Nevada.

What Letters Testamentary Actually Are

Letters Testamentary is a document issued by the probate court that formally authorizes a named individual — the executor of a will, called the "personal representative" in Nevada — to act on behalf of the deceased person's estate. It is, in essence, the estate's credentials: a court-issued document confirming that the named person has legal authority to collect assets, pay debts, manage estate property, and ultimately distribute what remains to the beneficiaries named in the will.

The related document, Letters of Administration, serves the identical function in intestate cases — when the deceased died without a will, or when the named executor in a will is unable, unwilling, or ineligible to serve. The court appoints an administrator (typically a surviving spouse, adult child, or other close relative) and issues Letters of Administration granting the same authority.

Both documents carry the court's seal and the signature of the court clerk. They are official documents, not informal letters.

Why No One Will Help You Without Them

The practical importance of Letters Testamentary cannot be overstated. Institutions of all kinds are legally required to protect the assets of deceased individuals — they cannot simply release those assets to anyone who claims to be a family member or named executor. Until you present Letters Testamentary, you will encounter the following walls:

Banks and credit unions will not close accounts in the decedent's name, release funds, or add your signature authority to estate accounts. They will ask for Letters Testamentary and will often require a certified copy for their own records.

Brokerage firms and retirement account custodians will not transfer investment accounts or release funds without court authorization. Many financial institutions will also require a certified copy that was issued within a specific recent period.

Real estate title companies will not transfer real property titled in the decedent's name without Letters Testamentary. A deed transferring real estate out of an estate cannot be recorded without the administrator's court-authorized signature.

Government agencies — including the Social Security Administration, Veterans Affairs, pension administrators, and state agencies — require Letters before releasing benefits or authorizing any account changes.

Courts and opposing parties in pending litigation need proof that the estate's representative is court-authorized before they will recognize your standing to continue or initiate legal proceedings on behalf of the estate.

The pattern is consistent: Letters Testamentary are required everywhere. Obtain certified copies — not just the original — because many institutions keep the copy they receive.

How to Obtain Letters Testamentary in Nevada

Obtaining Letters Testamentary in Nevada is a court process that requires specific filings and a hearing. Here is the sequence:

Step 1: File a Petition for Probate

The process begins with filing a Petition for Probate in the district court in the county where the deceased was domiciled at the time of death. In Clark County (Las Vegas), this is the Eighth Judicial District Court, Probate Division.

The petition must include: the original will (if one exists), a certified copy of the death certificate, identification of all beneficiaries named in the will and all legal heirs (even if they are not beneficiaries under the will), a description of the estate's assets and their estimated value, and a request for the proposed executor to be formally appointed.

Step 2: Notify Interested Parties

Nevada law requires that all interested parties — beneficiaries named in the will, legal heirs under intestate succession, and any known creditors — be given notice of the probate petition and the scheduled hearing. Notice requirements differ depending on the circumstances; a document preparer or attorney can advise on the specific notice requirements for your petition.

Step 3: Court Hearing

The court schedules a hearing on the petition for probate. If there are no objections, the hearing is typically brief. The court will review the will (if any), confirm that the proposed executor is qualified and willing to serve, and admit the will to probate.

Step 4: Order Admitting Will and Issuing Letters

After the hearing, the court enters an Order admitting the will to probate and formally appointing the executor. The court clerk then issues the Letters Testamentary — the actual authorization document. This is what you take to the bank, the title company, the brokerage, and everywhere else.

Request multiple certified copies at the time of issuance. Certified copies carry the court's seal and are treated as originals by most institutions. The cost per certified copy is typically a few dollars, and having six to ten certified copies on hand from the start saves significant time and headache.

Understanding the Scope and Limitations of Your Authority

Letters Testamentary authorize you to act as the estate's representative — but within limits defined by Nevada probate law and, in some cases, by the court's specific orders.

You act in a fiduciary capacity. As executor or administrator, you have a legal obligation to act in the best interests of the beneficiaries and creditors, not in your own interest. Nevada law takes fiduciary duty seriously in the probate context.

Some transactions require court approval. Selling estate real property, compromising or settling claims against the estate above certain thresholds, borrowing money on behalf of the estate, or making significant investments of estate assets may require a separate court order. Do not assume Letters Testamentary authorize every possible action without limitation.

You are accountable to the court. Nevada probate courts maintain oversight of estate administration. You will be required to file an inventory of assets, periodic or final accountings showing how estate assets were managed, and ultimately a petition for final distribution and discharge before the estate can be closed.

How Long Are Letters Testamentary Valid?

In Nevada, Letters Testamentary are typically issued without a fixed expiration, but many institutions will require a certified copy that was issued relatively recently — sometimes within the past six to twelve months — if the administration is ongoing. If your Letters are outdated in the eyes of a particular institution, you may need to obtain a new certified copy from the court clerk, which is typically straightforward.

Some Letters may also include specific terms or limitations imposed by the court. Review your Letters carefully and retain the full probate file throughout the administration.

The Document Work Involved

Obtaining and using Letters Testamentary involves a meaningful amount of court paperwork: the petition for probate, the death certificate, heir and beneficiary declarations, notice filings, proposed orders, and the Letters themselves. Each document must be correctly formatted for the specific Nevada district court handling the matter. Once issued, the Letters will accompany nearly every communication you have on behalf of the estate for months or years.
