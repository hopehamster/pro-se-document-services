NRS 240A Explained: What Nevada's Document Preparer Law Means for You

February 10, 2026 · 5 min read

If you are considering working with a document preparation service in Nevada, you should know that the state has a specific regulatory framework governing these services — and that framework exists entirely to protect you. Nevada Revised Statutes Chapter 240A is the law that defines what a registered document preparer can and cannot do, what disclosures they are required to make, and what consumer protections apply to your engagement with them. Understanding it takes the guesswork out of choosing a compliant, accountable provider.

The Purpose of NRS Chapter 240A

Before Nevada enacted Chapter 240A, anyone could advertise document preparation services to the public with essentially no accountability. People paid for legal documents prepared by individuals with no professional oversight, no required disclosures, and no consumer protection mechanisms. Predictably, that created problems — from documents that didn't meet court standards to outright misrepresentation of services.

NRS Chapter 240A changed that by creating a formal registration requirement, mandatory consumer disclosures, written contract requirements, and enforcement authority. The law draws a clear line between registered, accountable document preparation professionals and unregulated operators — and it gives you clear standards for evaluating any service you consider working with.

What the Law Requires of Registered Preparers

Under NRS Chapter 240A, a registered legal document preparer in Nevada must comply with the following requirements:

Registration

A document preparer must register with the appropriate Nevada authority before advertising or providing services to the public. Registration is maintained on an ongoing basis and is subject to renewal requirements. You can verify any preparer's registration status.

Mandatory Disclosure Language

This is perhaps the most visible requirement. Under NRS 240A.150, a registered document preparer must prominently display required disclosure language on all advertising and public-facing materials. The disclosure states clearly that the preparer is not an attorney, cannot provide legal advice, and cannot represent clients in court.

This language is not optional, and it is not a warning sign — it is proof of compliance. Any legitimate registered document preparer will display this language. If a service doesn't show you the required disclosure, that is a red flag, not reassurance.

Written Contract Requirement

Before providing services, a registered document preparer must enter into a written contract with the client that specifies the services to be provided, the fees to be charged, and the terms of the engagement. You are entitled to a copy of this contract. If a service refuses to provide a written agreement or is vague about fees before work begins, that is a compliance failure.

Client Copy of All Documents

The client is entitled to receive copies of all documents prepared on their behalf. This is a basic accountability requirement — you should always have your own complete record of what was prepared and filed.

Record Retention

Registered preparers are required to maintain client records for a specified period. This creates an auditable trail and supports accountability.

What This Means for You as a Consumer

When you engage a registered document preparer under NRS 240A, you have enforceable legal protections. You are entitled to a written agreement, clear fee disclosure, copies of your documents, and the assurance that the service is operating within a regulated framework.

If a service operates without registration, refuses to provide a written contract, claims to provide legal advice in addition to document preparation, or does not display the required disclosure language — those are signs of a non-compliant operation. Reporting non-compliant operators to the Nevada Attorney General's office is an option available to consumers who encounter them.

How to Verify Registration

You can verify a document preparer's registration through the Nevada Secretary of State's business registration database, accessible online. A legitimate registered service will also provide their registration information directly without hesitation if you ask.

When evaluating any document preparation service, ask these questions:

  • Are you registered under NRS Chapter 240A?
  • Can I see your required disclosure?
  • Will you provide a written contract before we begin?
  • What are your fees, stated as a flat rate?

A compliant service answers all four questions immediately and without qualification.

The Experience Factor: Above and Beyond Compliance

NRS 240A sets the regulatory floor — registration and consumer disclosure are the minimum requirements. What separates a high-quality document preparation service from a basic one is the professional experience brought to that compliant framework.

Paralegals who have spent years in corporate legal departments and major law firms bring a level of drafting precision to registered document preparation that goes well beyond basic compliance. They know what a Clark County District Court clerk expects. They know what local formatting rules apply in the U.S. District Court of Nevada. They know what makes a filing survive scrutiny versus what gets it rejected on the first review.

When your case depends on documents being done correctly the first time, that depth of experience is not a luxury — it is the service.
