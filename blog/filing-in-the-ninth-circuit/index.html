Filing in the Ninth Circuit: What Pro Se Appellants Need to Know

April 4, 2026 · 8 min read

If you've lost a case in the U.S. District Court of Nevada — or received an unfavorable decision from a federal administrative agency — the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit is where your appeal goes. The Ninth Circuit is the largest federal appellate court in the country by geography and one of the busiest by caseload, covering Nevada, California, Arizona, Oregon, Washington, Idaho, Montana, Alaska, Hawaii, Guam, and the Northern Mariana Islands. Pro se appellants appear before the Ninth Circuit regularly, but succeeding requires a precise understanding of the court's procedural rules and what appellate review actually requires.

Understanding What an Appeal Is — and Isn't

One of the most important things to grasp before filing an appeal is that an appellate court is not a second chance to argue your case from scratch. The Ninth Circuit does not hold new trials, receive new evidence, or allow new witnesses. It reviews the record that was created in the district court — the transcripts, pleadings, motions, orders, and exhibits that are already part of the case file.

The Ninth Circuit's job is to determine whether the district court made a reversible legal or factual error. If you are appealing, you are arguing that the district court got the law wrong, or applied the correct law to the facts incorrectly, or abused its discretion in a ruling. "I disagree with the outcome" is not a legal argument on appeal. "The district court applied the wrong legal standard to my Fourth Amendment claim" is.

This distinction has a critically important implication: the quality of your district court filings determines what is in the record on appeal. If you failed to raise an argument in the district court, you have generally waived the right to raise it for the first time on appeal. The appellate record is built below.

The Notice of Appeal: The Starting Gun

An appeal begins with filing a Notice of Appeal. This is a brief, formally structured document filed with the clerk of the district court — not directly with the Ninth Circuit. It identifies the party filing the appeal, the judgment or order being appealed, and the court to which the appeal is being taken.

The timing of the notice of appeal is jurisdictional — miss the deadline, and the Ninth Circuit has no power to hear your case, period, regardless of how strong your arguments are.

In civil cases: The notice of appeal must be filed within 30 days of the entry of the judgment or order being appealed. If the United States or a federal agency is a party, this deadline is extended to 60 days.

In criminal cases: The notice of appeal must be filed within 14 days of the entry of judgment.

One important nuance: Certain post-judgment motions — a timely motion for new trial, a motion to alter or amend the judgment, a motion for judgment as a matter of law — toll (pause) the appeal deadline. The clock restarts from the date the district court rules on those motions. Make sure you understand how any pending post-judgment motions affect your appeal deadline.

The Appellate Record

After the Notice of Appeal is filed, the district court clerk transmits the record — all of the documents filed in the district court case — to the Ninth Circuit. This is called the "record on appeal."

If there were court hearings or trial proceedings, the transcripts of those proceedings may also be part of the record. As the appellant, you are responsible for ordering any transcripts you want the Ninth Circuit to consider, and for paying the transcription costs. Failure to include relevant transcripts can leave the appellate court without the evidentiary foundation to rule in your favor.

Standards of Review: How the Ninth Circuit Looks at Different Issues

Understanding the standard of review that applies to each issue in your appeal is essential for crafting a persuasive brief. The standard of review dictates how much deference the Ninth Circuit gives to the district court's ruling:

De novo — The Ninth Circuit reviews the issue fresh, with no deference to the district court's ruling. This standard applies to questions of law — whether the court applied the correct legal standard, whether the complaint stated a valid claim, whether a motion to dismiss was correctly decided. De novo review is the most favorable standard for appellants on legal issues.

Clear error — The Ninth Circuit will reverse the district court's factual findings only if they are "clearly erroneous" — meaning the appellate court is left with a definite and firm conviction that a mistake was made. This is a highly deferential standard; factual findings that are supported by evidence in the record are rarely reversed.

Abuse of discretion — Applied to rulings where the district court has discretion, such as evidentiary rulings, class certification decisions, and many procedural rulings. The Ninth Circuit will reverse only if the district court's decision was arbitrary, illogical, or based on legal error.

Identifying the correct standard of review for each issue and framing your argument accordingly is a hallmark of a well-constructed appellate brief.

The Opening Brief: The Core Document

The opening brief is the central document of your appeal. It is your opportunity to present your arguments to the three-judge panel that will decide your case. Ninth Circuit rules impose strict requirements on all briefs, including pro se briefs:

Format requirements:

  • Standard type size of 14-point proportional serif font, or 12-point monospaced font
  • Double-spaced text (except for headings and quotations)
  • One-inch margins on all sides
  • Consecutively numbered pages

Length limits:

  • Principal briefs: 14,000 words (or approximately 30 pages if using line count)
  • Shorter limits may be imposed by court order
  • The word count limit excludes the table of contents, table of authorities, signature block, and certificates

Required sections under FRAP Rule 28:

  1. Table of Contents
  2. Table of Authorities (cases, statutes, and other authorities cited)
  3. Jurisdictional Statement — basis for district court jurisdiction, basis for circuit court jurisdiction, timeliness of the appeal
  4. Statement of Issues Presented for Review
  5. Statement of the Case — procedural history and relevant facts, with citations to the record
  6. Summary of Argument
  7. Argument — with section headings corresponding to each issue, applicable standard of review stated, and legal argument with case citations
  8. Conclusion — a brief statement of the relief requested
  9. Certificate of Compliance with word/length limit
  10. Certificate of Service

The argument section is where your case is won or lost on appeal. It must be organized by issue, state the applicable standard of review for each issue, cite controlling Ninth Circuit and U.S. Supreme Court authority, and apply that authority to the specific facts of your case as reflected in the record. An argument that simply restates what happened in the district court, without engaging with legal standards and controlling case law, will not succeed.

The Briefing Schedule and Oral Argument

After the opening brief is filed, the appellee (the party who won in district court) files an answering brief. You then have the opportunity to file a reply brief responding to the answering brief's arguments. The Ninth Circuit sets deadlines for each brief after the record is docketed.

Most Ninth Circuit cases — including most pro se cases — are decided on the written record without oral argument. The court selects cases for oral argument based on their significance and the court's judgment about whether argument would assist decision-making. If oral argument is granted, pro se appellants are allowed to argue their case in person before the panel.

The Ninth Circuit's Pro Se Unit

The Ninth Circuit maintains a Pro Se Unit that processes self-represented litigant filings, provides informational materials about the appeal process, and answers general procedural questions. The court's website at ca9.uscourts.gov includes guides and resources specifically for pro se appellants. These are valuable resources — use them.

However, informational resources are not a substitute for correctly formatted, legally sound appellate documents. A brief that doesn't meet formatting requirements, doesn't include required sections, or doesn't engage with controlling legal authority will not advance your appeal regardless of how well-intentioned it is.

After the Ninth Circuit: The Path to the Supreme Court

If you lose at the Ninth Circuit, you may petition the U.S. Supreme Court for a writ of certiorari — a request for the Court to review the Ninth Circuit's decision. The Supreme Court grants certiorari in fewer than 2% of petitions received. Certiorari is typically granted when there is a conflict among the federal circuit courts on an important legal question, when a case presents a significant constitutional issue, or when a lower court has decided an important federal question in a way that conflicts with Supreme Court precedent.

A petition for writ of certiorari must comply with Supreme Court Rule 14 and carries its own formatting requirements and a 90-day filing deadline from the Ninth Circuit's judgment.
