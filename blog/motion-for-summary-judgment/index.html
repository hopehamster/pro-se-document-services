What Is a Motion for Summary Judgment and When Should You File One?

February 28, 2026 · 6 min read

A motion for summary judgment is one of the most powerful procedural tools in civil litigation — and one of the most technically demanding documents a pro se litigant will encounter. Understanding what it is, what it requires, and how courts evaluate it will help you either pursue it effectively when the facts are on your side or defend against it when the opposing party files one against you.

What Summary Judgment Actually Does

A motion for summary judgment (MSJ) is a request to the court to decide the case — or a specific issue within the case — without holding a trial. The argument is that there is no genuine dispute about the material facts, and based on those undisputed facts, one party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

The policy rationale is efficiency. Trials are expensive, time-consuming, and resource-intensive for courts, parties, and witnesses. If there are no facts actually in dispute — if the only real question is how the law applies to an agreed set of facts — a full trial wastes everyone's time. Summary judgment resolves those cases on the papers.

Either side can file for summary judgment. A plaintiff may file after discovery establishes that the undisputed facts clearly support their claims. A defendant may file arguing that even accepting everything the plaintiff alleges as true, no legally valid claim has been established.

The Legal Standard

Under Nevada Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 56, a court shall grant summary judgment if the moving party shows:

  1. There is no genuine issue as to any material fact, and
  2. The moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law

The same standard applies in federal court under Federal Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 56.

Let's unpack what those two elements actually mean:

"Material fact" — A fact is "material" if it could affect the outcome of the case under the applicable legal standard. Not every factual dispute qualifies. A dispute about irrelevant details doesn't create a genuine issue of material fact that defeats summary judgment.

"Genuine dispute" — A dispute is "genuine" if a reasonable jury could find in favor of the non-moving party based on the evidence in the record. The question is not whether there is any disagreement between the parties — the question is whether the evidence supporting the non-moving party's position is sufficient to send the matter to a jury.

When evaluating a summary judgment motion, the court views all evidence and draws all reasonable inferences in favor of the non-moving party. Summary judgment is not appropriate when the record contains evidence that could reasonably support a verdict for either side.

The Document Package Required for an MSJ

A motion for summary judgment is not a single document — it is a package of related filings, each of which has its own requirements:

The Motion Itself

The motion is the lead document. It identifies the claims or issues on which you seek judgment, sets out the legal standard, and argues that the undisputed facts entitle you to judgment as a matter of law. It should be organized clearly with a statement of the relief requested, a memorandum of points and authorities (legal argument with case citations), and a conclusion.

Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (SUMF)

In Nevada district courts and federal courts, the moving party is required to file a separate document listing, in numbered paragraphs, each material fact that the moving party contends is not in dispute. Each fact must be followed by a citation to the specific evidence in the record that establishes it — an exhibit number, a specific page and line of a deposition transcript, a specific paragraph of an affidavit.

The SUMF is critically important. Courts use it as the roadmap for evaluating the motion. A poorly organized or unsupported SUMF is one of the most common reasons motions for summary judgment are denied even when the moving party has a strong substantive position.

Supporting Exhibits

The factual claims in your SUMF must be backed by admissible evidence attached to your motion as exhibits. Acceptable supporting evidence typically includes:

  • Affidavits or declarations made under penalty of perjury from individuals with personal knowledge
  • Deposition transcripts with the relevant pages attached
  • Business records authenticated by declaration
  • Contracts, agreements, or documents already in evidence
  • Responses to requests for admission

Hearsay documents not accompanied by a proper authentication declaration are frequently challenged and excluded.

Memorandum of Points and Authorities

The legal brief accompanying your motion must cite the applicable legal standard, cite relevant case law supporting your position, and apply that law to the undisputed facts. Courts expect specific case citations, not general legal propositions. A memorandum that asserts legal conclusions without supporting case authority is not persuasive.

Defending Against a Motion for Summary Judgment

If the opposing party files a motion for summary judgment against you, your response is due within a set deadline — typically 21 days in Nevada district court, though local rules may specify differently and courts often set specific briefing schedules.

Your response must:

  • Identify specifically which of the moving party's "undisputed" facts you actually dispute
  • Provide evidence from the record supporting your dispute of those facts
  • File your own statement of genuine disputes of material fact with record citations
  • Make legal arguments for why the motion should be denied

A non-response to a summary judgment motion, or a vague response without evidentiary support, is treated as a concession that the stated facts are undisputed. Courts routinely grant summary judgment motions against pro se defendants who do not file adequate oppositions.

Whether to File: A Practical Consideration

The decision to file a motion for summary judgment in your specific case is one that involves legal strategy — which means it is a question for a licensed attorney if you have one, or a determination you make based on your own assessment of your case if you are proceeding entirely pro se. Document preparation services can prepare a technically correct MSJ package once you've determined that's the right move; they cannot advise you on whether that determination is sound.

What we can tell you is that a technically defective MSJ — one with an unsupported SUMF, missing exhibits, or a memorandum that doesn't engage with the applicable legal standard — will be denied regardless of how strong your underlying position is. The documents have to be right.
