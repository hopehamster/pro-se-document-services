What Is Federal Habeas Corpus and Can You File Without an Attorney?

April 1, 2026 · 8 min read

The writ of habeas corpus is one of the oldest and most fundamental protections in Anglo-American law. Traceable to the Magna Carta and carried into U.S. law through Article I of the Constitution — which expressly prohibits suspension of the writ "unless when in Cases of Rebellion or Invasion the public Safety may require it" — habeas corpus is the legal mechanism by which a person challenging the legality of their custody can bring that challenge before a federal court. For individuals who believe their conviction or sentence violates the Constitution or federal law, federal habeas corpus is often the last viable avenue for relief. This post explains what it is, what it requires, and what pro se petitioners need to understand before filing.

The Two Primary Federal Habeas Statutes

28 U.S.C. § 2254 — State Prisoners

The most common form of federal habeas corpus is brought under 28 U.S.C. § 2254, which allows a person held in state custody to petition the federal district court for relief on the grounds that they are in custody in violation of the Constitution, laws, or treaties of the United States.

Section 2254 is available to state prisoners after their direct appeal rights have been exhausted. It is not a direct appeal — it is a collateral attack on a conviction or sentence, based on constitutional claims that the state courts failed to adequately address.

28 U.S.C. § 2255 — Federal Prisoners

A person convicted in federal court and sentenced by a federal judge may challenge their conviction or sentence through a § 2255 motion to vacate, set aside, or correct the sentence. Section 2255 is filed in the district court that imposed the sentence, and it is the primary vehicle for federal prisoners to raise constitutional claims post-conviction.

The most common grounds for § 2255 relief include: ineffective assistance of counsel (under the Strickland v. Washington standard), a Fourth Amendment claim that evidence was unconstitutionally obtained, a claim that the sentence was imposed in violation of federal law, and claims based on newly enacted or newly applied constitutional rules.

Common Grounds for Federal Habeas Relief

Both § 2254 and § 2255 petitions are based on federal constitutional claims. Common grounds include:

Ineffective assistance of counsel — Under Strickland v. Washington, a defendant who received constitutionally deficient performance from their trial counsel that resulted in prejudice (meaning there is a reasonable probability the outcome would have been different but for the attorney's errors) may seek habeas relief. This is by far the most frequently raised habeas ground.

Unconstitutional search and seizure — Evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment may be challenged, though the Stone v. Powell doctrine limits Fourth Amendment claims in § 2254 petitions when the petitioner had a full and fair opportunity to litigate those claims in state court.

Brady violations — The prosecution's failure to disclose material exculpatory evidence in violation of Brady v. Maryland is a recognized constitutional violation that can form the basis of a habeas claim.

Actual innocence — While not an independent constitutional claim by itself in most circuits, actual innocence can serve as a gateway to review of otherwise procedurally barred claims, and in some circumstances may itself support relief.

Newly recognized constitutional rules — Under Teague v. Lane, new rules of constitutional law generally do not apply retroactively to cases already final on direct review, with narrow exceptions for rules that place certain conduct beyond the power of criminal law or that constitute a watershed change in criminal procedure.

The Exhaustion Requirement

For § 2254 petitions, federal courts require that you first exhaust all available state court remedies before bringing your claims to federal court. This means you must present your constitutional claims to the highest state court with jurisdiction to review them and receive a decision on those claims.

Exhaustion is not satisfied by simply mentioning a constitutional issue in passing. Courts require that you "fairly present" each claim — meaning you raised the same legal theory and the same facts in state court. A claim that was argued differently in state court, or that relies on a federal constitutional theory never presented to the state courts, is generally considered unexhausted.

If any of your claims are unexhausted when you file in federal court, the court may dismiss the entire petition without prejudice to allow you to exhaust in state court — but only if the AEDPA statute of limitations hasn't run in the meantime. This creates a serious practical trap for petitioners who do not carefully coordinate their state and federal post-conviction timelines.

The AEDPA Statute of Limitations: The Most Consequential Deadline in Post-Conviction Law

The Anti-Terrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act of 1996 (AEDPA) imposed a one-year statute of limitations on federal habeas corpus petitions. This deadline is among the most strictly enforced in all of federal litigation — courts have very little equitable flexibility to extend it, and missing it is almost always fatal.

For § 2254 petitions, the one-year clock typically begins running from the date the conviction became final on direct review — meaning the date the U.S. Supreme Court denied certiorari on direct appeal, or the date the deadline for seeking certiorari expired after the state supreme court decided the direct appeal.

Tolling Provisions

The AEDPA limitations period is tolled (paused) during the time a "properly filed" application for state post-conviction review is pending in state court. This means that if you file a timely state post-conviction petition before your federal year expires, the federal clock stops while the state court considers it and restarts when the state court issues a final decision.

However, tolling only works if your state petition was "properly filed" — a petition filed out of time under state procedural rules may not qualify for tolling, meaning the federal clock keeps running even while you wait for the state court to reject your late filing.

Additional exceptions include newly discovered factual claims (one year from the date the new facts could have been discovered with due diligence) and newly recognized constitutional rights made retroactively applicable by the Supreme Court (one year from the date of the new decision).

Actual Innocence Exception

The Supreme Court in McQuiggin v. Perkins (2013) recognized a narrow actual innocence gateway that allows a court to consider an otherwise time-barred petition if the petitioner presents new evidence that makes it more likely than not that no reasonable juror would have convicted them. This is an exceptionally high bar that few petitioners satisfy.

Procedural Default: The Other Major Bar to Federal Review

Even a timely, exhausted habeas petition may be procedurally defaulted. A claim is procedurally defaulted when the state courts declined to reach the merits of the claim based on an independent and adequate state procedural ground — for example, because the petitioner failed to raise the claim in a timely manner under state procedural rules.

Federal courts will not review a procedurally defaulted claim unless the petitioner can show: (1) cause for the default and actual prejudice, or (2) that failure to consider the claim will result in a fundamental miscarriage of justice (essentially, actual innocence). The cause-and-prejudice exception has been expanded by Martinez v. Ryan (2012) to allow ineffective assistance of post-conviction counsel to serve as "cause" for the default of an ineffective assistance of trial counsel claim in some circumstances.

Can You File Without an Attorney?

Yes. There is no requirement of counsel in federal civil habeas corpus proceedings under § 2254 or § 2255. Pro se habeas petitions are common in the U.S. District Court of Nevada, and courts apply the liberal reading standard from Haines v. Kerner to pro se filings.

The Administrative Office of the U.S. Courts has a standardized form for § 2254 petitions (Form AO 241) and § 2255 motions (Form AO 243) that pro se petitioners are often required to use or may choose to use as a guide. These forms prompt you to provide the information the court needs, including case history, grounds for relief, exhaustion status, and prior petition history.

However, the procedural complexity of federal habeas — the exhaustion requirement, the AEDPA deadline, tolling calculations, procedural default doctrine, and the requirement that constitutional claims be specifically and precisely articulated — makes this one of the most technically demanding documents a pro se litigant can attempt. A habeas petition that is dismissed on procedural grounds — or that fails to properly articulate the constitutional basis for a potentially valid claim — can permanently foreclose relief.

Professional document preparation for a federal habeas petition ensures that the petition is correctly formatted, uses the proper form, identifies the grounds for relief clearly and with appropriate constitutional specificity, demonstrates exhaustion, and complies with local rules. On a matter with stakes this high, that precision matters.
